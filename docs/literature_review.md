# Algorithmic Jazz Improvisation: A Research Note

*Trad-Four project background reading*

---

## 1. Overview

The computational generation of jazz improvisation has been studied since the late 1980s and has developed along five broadly distinct paradigms: rule-based and grammar-based systems, statistical sequence models, concatenative synthesis via factor oracles, evolutionary algorithms, and deep learning approaches. A separate analytical research program — the Jazzomat/Weimar group — has produced a corpus and toolkit that underpins several of these systems. This note surveys the main contributions in each paradigm, with attention to their relevance to the Trad-Four architecture.

---

## 2. Rule-based and Grammar-based Systems

The foundational theoretical contribution is Johnson-Laird's argument that jazz improvisation can be modeled as a generative grammar over harmonic constraints \cite{johnsonlaird1991jazz}. This framework distinguishes between a harmonic skeleton (the chord progression), a set of scale and chord-tone constraints derivable from that skeleton, and a generative process that produces melodic sequences consistent with those constraints. The insight that rhythm and pitch can be generated by separate but coupled processes is central to this tradition.

Keller's **Impro-Visor** \cite{keller2004improvisor} is the most fully realized implementation of this framework. Its grammar formalism uses weighted probabilistic context-free rules over an alphabet of typed rhythmic events — chord tones (`C`), color/tension tones (`L`), scale tones (`S`), approach tones (`A`), and rests (`R`) — to generate a typed rhythm skeleton. A separate pitch-filling stage then samples from the harmonically appropriate note pool for each typed slot, weighted by contour (preferring stepwise motion). The grammar is interpretable, compact, and directly learnable from transcribed solos via rule-counting over labeled sequences \cite{gillick2010machine}.

A significant extension is Impro-Visor's trading framework, which generates phrases in alternation with a human player — the direct inspiration for Trad-Four's name and core use case.

**Relevance to Trad-Four:** The grammar-based two-stage architecture (rhythm skeleton + pitch filling) is directly adopted in Trad-Four's Phase 2 and Phase 3.

---

## 3. Markov and Statistical Sequence Models

The landmark system in this paradigm is Pachet's **Continuator** \cite{pachet2003continuator}. Rather than encoding musical knowledge explicitly, the Continuator learns a variable-order Markov model from a performer's playing and generates stylistically consistent continuations in real time. The variable-order design — dynamically selecting the longest matching context available in the learned model — allows the system to capture both short-range note-to-note tendencies and longer idiomatic patterns without fixing a context window in advance.

A key design choice in the Continuator is treating harmony as a soft constraint rather than a hard one: the Markovian generation is weighted toward harmonically consonant notes, but not forced. This naturally permits "outside" playing — a musically authentic behavior that hard-constrained systems cannot easily reproduce. A later extension \cite{pachet2003continuator2} added bebop-specific rules, side-slipping (locally dissonant but semantically recoverable passages), and non-Markovian global constraints.

From the Weimar research group, Frieler developed an analysis-by-synthesis model based on the **Weimar Bebop Alphabet (WBA)** \cite{frieler2019wba} — a compact vocabulary of melodic atoms derived empirically from the Weimar Jazz Database. A key finding is that bebop lines decompose into short atoms that follow at most a first-order Markov model at the phrase level, substantially simplifying the generative model. A 2025 study using the same database \cite{weimar2025markov} confirmed that low-order Markov chains strike a favorable balance of interpretability, computational efficiency, and data efficiency for phrase-level rhythmic generation.

**Relevance to Trad-Four:** The Markov approach is a viable alternative or complement to the PCFG for Phase 2 grammar induction, particularly for initial prototyping where a transition matrix over note-type/duration states is easier to implement and inspect than a full CFG.

---

## 4. Factor Oracle and Concatenative Synthesis

The **OMax** system \cite{assayag2006omax}, developed at IRCAM by Assayag, Dubnov, and colleagues, takes a fundamentally different approach: rather than generating new notes, it navigates and recombines segments from a musical corpus using a **factor oracle** — a compact finite-state automaton that indexes the corpus and enables efficient retrieval of the most contextually appropriate continuation. The result sounds stylistically consistent because it *is* the source material, reorganized.

OMax's philosophical position is notable: it treats improvisation as navigation through a space of previously heard material, not as synthesis from abstract rules. This aligns with cognitive accounts of expert improvisation as the recombination of internalized "licks" and patterns \cite{berliner1994thinking}.

However, OMax has documented musical weaknesses: it lacks tension-release structure and voice leading awareness, and its improvisations show limitations in rhythm, groove, and large-scale coherence \cite{surges2013evaluating}. Successor systems — **ImproteK** \cite{chemillier2004improtek}, **DYCI2** \cite{nika2017dyci2}, and **Somax2** \cite{bjorn2020somax2} — progressively added scenario-based harmonic guidance and multi-level temporal structure on top of the factor oracle foundation.

**Relevance to Trad-Four:** The factor oracle paradigm is not directly adopted, but the underlying insight — that a player's style is a vocabulary of recombined fragments — motivates Trad-Four's lick library extension (Phase 2e).

## 5. Evolutionary and Genetic Algorithm Approaches

**GenJam** \cite{biles1994genjam} is the canonical evolutionary approach. It maintains hierarchically related populations of melodic ideas (measures and phrases) that are mapped to pitches via scales suggested by the current chord progression. A human mentor provides real-time fitness feedback during live performance, driving selection and crossover across generations.

The system is conceptually elegant — improvisation as fitness-guided search — but faces a fundamental bottleneck: the human mentor must evaluate each individual, limiting practical population sizes and generation counts \cite{biles2007genjam}. Autonomous GenJam addressed this by seeding populations from a lick database and automating fitness evaluation, making the genetic machinery a recombination engine over pre-existing musical material — similar in spirit to the factor oracle.

**Relevance to Trad-Four:** Not directly adopted. The interactive fitness paradigm is interesting for future active-trading extensions but adds human-in-the-loop complexity that is out of scope for the current system.

## 6. Deep Learning Approaches

### 6.1 LSTM-based Models

Eck and Schmidhuber \cite{eck2002blues} were the first to apply LSTMs to blues improvisation, demonstrating that recurrent networks could maintain harmonic structure over longer time horizons than vanilla RNNs.

The most directly relevant work for Trad-Four is Johnson, Keller, and Weintraut's **Product of Experts** model \cite{johnson2017jazz}, which describes the LSTM architecture integrated into Impro-Visor 9.0 (see Section 7 below). The **JazzGAN** system \cite{trieu2018jazzgan} from the same laboratory used RNN-based GANs to generate monophonic jazz melodies over given chord progressions, with particular strength in handling frequent key changes and unconventional rhythms.

Wang et al. \cite{wang2019hybrid} proposed a hybrid approach combining music theory grammar with LSTM learning, extracting interval, duration, and note-category information from jazz MIDI files and training an LSTM to learn transition probabilities between note types. This hybrid is the most architecturally similar to Trad-Four's design.

### 6.2 Transformer-based Models

More recent work applies attention-based sequence models to symbolic music. The **Jazz Transformer** \cite{wu2020jazztransformer} adapts the Transformer architecture to jazz lead sheet generation. **MINGUS** \cite{madaghiele2021mingus} uses a sequence-to-sequence Transformer conditioned on chord progressions. These models benefit from longer context windows than LSTMs but require substantially more training data and are less interpretable.

### 6.3 Assessment

Deep learning models excel at capturing surface statistical regularities but face consistent criticisms: lack of long-range structural coherence, opacity (the learned representations are not musically interpretable), and data hunger. As Chao \cite{chao2024improvisorcritique} notes in an analysis of Impro-Visor's own LSTM adoption, the grammar-based approach describes musical reality at far less storage and computational cost, and Impro-Visor's developers have subsequently released many more grammars than connectomes.

**Relevance to Trad-Four:** Deep learning is not adopted in the current architecture, but the hybrid grammar + LSTM approach of Wang et al. is a natural future direction once the grammar induction pipeline is established.

## 7. Impro-Visor's LSTM Architecture in Detail

The LSTM system introduced in Impro-Visor 9.0 \cite{johnson2017jazz} is architecturally distinct from the grammar engine and warrants separate treatment. Training is performed by the external Python repository `lstmprovisor-python` using Theano; the resulting network weights are serialized into a `.ctome` (connectome) file which Impro-Visor loads at runtime via the `imp/lstm` Java package.

The network architecture is a **Product of Experts** consisting of two independent LSTM sub-networks:

- **The interval expert** encodes pitch as a contour (interval relative to the previous note), using a circles-of-thirds representation originally described by Franklin \cite{franklin2004recurrent}. This expert captures melodic shape independently of absolute pitch.
- **The chord expert** encodes pitch relative to the current chord (chord-relative encoding), capturing harmonic function independently of melodic shape.

The two experts produce independent probability distributions over the next note; these are multiplied (the product-of-experts combination) to produce a joint distribution that satisfies both melodic and harmonic preferences simultaneously. The network also outputs explicit rest and sustain probabilities at each time step.

Training operates on 4-bar segments of `.ls` leadsheet files with a half-note feature period (24 units), using the ADAM optimizer. The trained connectome is loaded by Impro-Visor's Java `imp/lstm` package, which runs the network forward at generation time to produce note-by-note probability distributions, samples from them, and feeds the output back as the next input.

The practical outcome, as noted in \cite{chao2024improvisorcritique}, is that the LSTM and grammar models are difficult to distinguish perceptually, but the LSTM connectomes are substantially larger and slower to train, which explains why grammar development has continued to dominate Impro-Visor's recent evolution.

## 8. The Jazzomat / Weimar Research Program

Distinct from the generation systems above, the Jazzomat project \cite{pfleiderer2017jazzomat} at HfM Weimar is primarily analytical, investigating the creative processes underlying jazz improvisation with statistical and computational methods. Its primary outputs are:

- The **Weimar Jazz Database (WJD)** \cite{weimar2021wjd} — 456 annotated jazz solos with beat-level note function labels, available in SQLite format.
- **MeloSpyLib** — a Python library for melodic analysis.
- The **Weimar Bebop Alphabet (WBA)** \cite{frieler2019wba} — an empirically derived vocabulary of melodic atoms. The WBA taxonomy distinguishes nine main playing idea types (line, lick, theme, quote, melody, rhythm, expressive, fragment, void), with lick and line types covering approximately 75% of all solo durations. This taxonomy is directly relevant to defining grammar non-terminals in Phase 2 of Trad-Four.

## 9. Open Problems and Trad-Four's Position

Across all paradigms, several problems remain consistently unsolved:

**Long-range phrase structure.** No system reliably generates the arc of tension and release across 8–32 bars that characterizes convincing human improvisation. OMax lacks it by design; grammars encode it only crudely; deep learning models learn local statistics but not global shape.

**Stylistic specificity at the individual level.** Most systems learn genre-level statistics (bebop, modal jazz) rather than individual player fingerprints. Exceptions — the Continuator learning from a live performer, GenJam's mentor feedback — require real-time human involvement.

**Evaluation.** There is no agreed metric for improvisation quality. Most papers use informal listening tests or note-function statistics (percentage of chord tones, etc.). The Weimar group's analytical framework is the most rigorous available.

Trad-Four's architecture addresses the first two problems directly: Phase 3c (phrase shape controller) targets long-range structure, and Phase 2's corpus induction from individual player corpora in the WJD targets individual stylistic specificity. The evaluation problem is left for future work.
